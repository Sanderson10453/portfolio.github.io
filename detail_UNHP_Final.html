Gambling with Homes, or Investing in Communities

We sought out to understand patterns of speculation over time, and how speculation impacts residents through building violations and evictions.

Housing speculation drives evictions and poor housing maintenance quality

When NYC landlords use their buildings as a piggy bank, quickly racking up debt, we found statistical significant evidence that building have higher violations and evictions, especially in communities of color. For our paper on how NYC landlord practices influence building violations and evictions, I lead the data mining process and cleaning of all affordable housing subsidy and evictions data, then ran logistic regressions. Additionally, I wrote grouped and aggregated the debts for the portfolios of: Ved Parkash, Cooper Square, Los Sures/Southside United HDFC, and Banana Kelly Community Improvement Association

Process

In regards to the affordable housing subsidies, we needed to know the start and end days of various subsidies in order to know if they had an effect on violations and evictions. In order to make this data useable for our analysis, I had to transform the data to be wide and ensure it was in a date format; to do so, I ran a loop to create proper start and end dates for the data - this code is shown in the second image. Once this data had been transformed, I moved on to the evictions data. For the evictions data, we needed to have quarterly counts of evictions for each BBL. This meant transforming long data to wide data with a quarterly count - to do this, I created another loop that is shown in the third image. For the analysis on evictions, it was also necessary to know the reason for eviction because different eviction reasons hold different information about a building's state. Given this, I wrote another loop for the eviction data that would aggregate quarterly evictions by reason for eviction - this is shown in the 4th image.
